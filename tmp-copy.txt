[Heading3] Card 1
Why your content is being read more than you think Category: Search & Discovery 30 January 2026
Something unusual showed up in the analytics for NZ Performance Car during 2025. Human traffic to the site fell — down from around 30,000 billable visits per month to 17,000. At the same time, total requests nearly doubled, from 45,000 to 80,000 per month. By the end of the year, bandwidth had jumped from 126GB to 196GB per month.
Those two things should not move in opposite directions. If fewer people are visiting, bandwidth should fall, not climb by more than 50 per cent. So what was pulling all that data?
The answer, in short, is AI.
What is actually happening
AI-assisted search tools — Google's AI Overviews, Bing's Copilot, ChatGPT with live search enabled, and a growing list of others — do not work the way traditional search does. When a user asks an AI-powered search tool a question, the tool often fetches live content from indexed pages in real time to construct its answer. It does not wait for a human to click; it goes and gets the page itself.
This process is driven by what AI engineers call query fan-out — the mechanism by which AI search engines break a single user question into multiple related sub-queries, crawling a wider range of sources before assembling a synthesised answer. For topically deep, niche content, this means a single user query can trigger multiple fetches across several pages of a specialist site.
From a server's perspective, that looks exactly like a visit. Requests are made, images are loaded, bandwidth is consumed. But no human ever arrived. No pageview is recorded. No session begins.
For image-heavy, high-authority niche content — the kind that sits on specialist sites like NZ Performance Car — this effect is amplified. Each fetch can pull multiple megabytes of images alongside the text. Multiply that across thousands of AI-assisted queries and the bandwidth numbers start to make sense.
This is not unique to one site. Research from Ahrefs and Semrush has flagged increasing AI crawler activity on niche, topically authoritative sites since mid-2023. Gartner's 2023 projection that traditional search would lose 25 per cent of its volume by 2026 is now playing out. SparkToro's 2024 study, drawing on clickstream data from millions of actual searches, found that nearly 60 per cent of Google searches in the US and EU ended without a single click to any external website.
The picture in New Zealand is consistent with these global signals. A 2025 survey by IAB New Zealand found that 48 per cent of respondents had used ChatGPT, and 39 per cent had used an AI chatbot in place of a traditional search engine at least once. While the survey was limited to upper North Island adults, the directional signal is clear: AI-assisted discovery is no longer an emerging behaviour. It is already part of how New Zealanders research and make decisions.
The clicks are not disappearing. The discovery is just happening somewhere else.
What this means for content
For years the measure of content performance was straightforward: traffic, clicks, time on page. Those metrics made sense when search meant a list of blue links and a human choosing one.
That model is breaking down. A user asking an AI assistant which performance parts suit their build, or which workshop in Auckland specialises in their make, may never see a traditional search results page at all. Where your business might once have appeared in the organic results, it may not surface now — unless the question is specific enough to reference your brand or service directly. But the AI will still produce an answer, and that answer will be drawn from somewhere.
The sites and brands it draws from are the ones with genuine authority: specific, credible, well-structured content in clearly defined niches. Broad content produced at volume is not what AI tools reach for. They reach for depth.
Where Via Media sits in this
This creates two distinct ways for a brand to be present in AI-assisted discovery. The first is earned — being mentioned in trusted editorial environments, reviews, and specialist publications, so that AI systems recognise your brand as a credible voice in the category. The second is owned — creating content specific enough and authoritative enough to be cited directly in AI-generated answers.
Via Media operates in both. To test the principle, we ran an AI search on a subject directly relevant to a client in the performance aftermarket sector. Of the eight citations the AI returned to construct its answer, two referenced NZ Performance Car editorial content featuring that client's brand and services. The content we had created for them was actively being used to inform purchase decisions — without a single click ever reaching their website.
That matters beyond discovery. AI-assisted search is increasingly the full purchase journey. Users are researching, comparing, and narrowing their choices inside the chat interface itself — then following up to find the best price or nearest stockist. A brand that is present in the cited sources is in that conversation from the start. A brand that is not, simply isn't there.
The implication for brands
If your brand is not present in the environments where AI-assisted discovery is happening, you are not in the consideration set. A potential customer asking an AI assistant for a recommendation will get an answer — but it will not include brands that have not built a presence in credible, niche content channels.
This is not a future concern.
The shift is already measurable. The brands building content authority now are the ones that will be referenced when it matters.
Niche authority is not something you can buy with broad spend — but it is something you can build, systematically, with the right media partner.
[Heading3] Card 2
One media partner. Every part of the campaign.
Category: Media & Strategy
23 February 2026
Running a marketing campaign in 2026 is more complicated than it has ever been — not because the goal has changed, but because the number of moving parts has.
Over the past two decades, the tools available to marketers have multiplied. Specialist content creators, influencer networks, digital media channels, paid social platforms, programmatic advertising, SEO, AEO — each one a legitimate option, each one its own discipline, its own supplier, its own budget line. For a brand trying to reach a specific audience with a coherent message, assembling all of those parts into something that actually works together is a significant undertaking.
In a small market like New Zealand, that problem is even more challenging. The pool of credible specialist voices in any given sector is shallow. Agencies capable of managing the full stack are generalist by necessity. Clients who need to reach niche audiences often find that the specialist options are hard to find, expensive to coordinate, and unconvincing in practice — because the distance between the intended message and the audience it needs to reach is crowded with handoffs, margin layers, and suppliers who don’t share context.
The coordination problem
The way a typical campaign gets built today looks something like this. A brand engages an agency to develop the strategy and media plan. The agency sources a content producer or engages an influencer with the right audience fit. The content is created, approved, and handed to a media team to place and amplify. Paid spend goes through platform tools managed by yet another party.
At every handoff, something is lost. The content creator doesn’t know the media environment. The media team didn’t brief the content. The influencer’s audience may overlap with the target — or may not. Nothing is native to the environment it eventually lands in, and audiences in high-trust specialist communities are perceptive about that disconnect.
The result is campaigns that feel assembled rather than coherent, and a client who has paid multiple margin layers for the privilege.
A different way of working
Via Media holds every part of that model in a single, integrated operation — built on thirty years of communicating with NZ automotive audiences and understanding what engages them.
The best way to explain what that means in practice is through an analogy. Imagine an influencer coming to you — someone with a large, loyal, specialist following who genuinely understands your product, your category, and your customer. They create content that resonates because they live in the same world as your audience. Their followers trust them. When they endorse something, it connects.
Now give that influencer the content production capability of a full agency. Give them owned media channels — platforms they have built and operated for decades — so they can publish that content natively in environments the audience already seeks out. Give them the paid amplification infrastructure to extend that reach beyond their organic following, with targeting built on first-party audience data. Then put it all under one roof, on one brief, for one fee.
That is what working with Via Media looks like. The content is created by the team that built the audience. It is published in environments that the audience already trusts. The paid media that surrounds and amplifies it is managed through channels we own and operate, so the message stays coherent from the first piece of content through to the last impression.
For brands wanting to reach NZ automotive audiences — enthusiasts, trade professionals, and category decision-makers — the environment already exists. Content created for them, in the channels they already choose to be in, by the team that built those channels.
Why presence matters more than campaigns
The instinct for many businesses is to invest in a campaign — a defined push, a specific period, a measurable result — and then step back. That approach made sense when advertising was expensive and episodic by nature.
It makes less sense now. Audience trust is built through consistent presence, not periodic appearances. Search authority compounds over time. A brand that shows up regularly in a trusted content community environment becomes part of the landscape that the audience navigates. A brand that appears once and disappears does not.
We work with clients across a range of investment levels, and the principle scales. A smaller operator in a regional market and a national brand running a full campaign are working toward the same thing: consistent, credible presence in the environments where their customers are already paying attention. The difference is scope, not approach.
The simpler version
The marketing landscape got complicated because the parts that used to sit together were pulled apart. The most effective way to work in that landscape is not to reassemble those parts every time you run a campaign — it is to work with a partner for whom they were never separate in the first place.
[Heading3] Card 3
The gatekeeper returns
Category: Media & Audience
11 March 2026
There was a time when getting a message to a mass audience required permission.
Not legal permission — social permission. The kind granted by an editor who decided what was worth printing, a broadcaster who chose what made the evening bulletin, a publisher who determined which voices deserved a platform. These were the gatekeepers: the curators who stood between the volume of things that could be said and the much smaller selection of things that actually reached people.
They were imperfect. They were sometimes self-serving. They reflected the biases of the institutions they worked within. But they performed a function that audiences relied on without fully appreciating it — they filtered signal from noise. They made choices so that audiences didn't have to.
The gates come down
The first wave of the internet changed that permanently, and mostly for the better. Publishing became democratic. Anyone with something to say could find an audience. Niche communities that had never had a dedicated voice suddenly had dozens of them — forums, blogs, specialist sites, fan channels — each one serving an audience that the mainstream had never bothered to reach.
Audiences fragmented into self-selected communities built around specific interests, and within those communities, trust was earned differently. Not through institutional authority, but through demonstrated knowledge, consistency, and genuine participation. The gatekeeper was replaced by the algorithm — and then, increasingly, by the audience itself, curating its own feed from the abundance now available to it.
For a while, that worked reasonably well. The feed was noisy, but navigable. The effort of finding good content in a specific niche was rewarded often enough to justify it.
The noise changes character
What has changed is not the volume of content — that has been growing for twenty years. What has changed is who, or what, is producing it.
AI content generation has altered the equation in a way that mere volume never did. The content now filling feeds is not just abundant — it is personalised, plausible, and produced at a scale that no human content-creation operation could match. Algorithms surface what your history suggests you want. AI tools make it faster and cheaper to produce more of exactly that. The loop tightens.
The result is a feed that feels responsive but is increasingly circular — more of what you have already seen, refined and repackaged, with no editorial intelligence applied to whether it is actually worth your time. This is what has been termed, with some accuracy, "AI slop": content that exists not because someone had something to say, but because the system determined you were likely to engage with it.
Navigating that is no longer a manageable inconvenience. It is becoming the defining challenge of consuming information.
The function that never went away
Here is what the past two decades have demonstrated: audiences did not stop wanting curation. They stopped relying solely on the institutions that provided it and found alternatives — creators, community voices, specialist channels — that offered the same function with greater authenticity and less institutional distance.
The language around this has shifted. We talk now about trust and authenticity, about micro-communities and long-term creator relationships, about engagement that runs deeper than impressions. But the underlying need is the same one the original gatekeepers were serving: help me find what is worth my attention, from a source I have reason to trust.
That function is not obsolete. It is more valuable than it has been in years — because the noise it is filtering has never been louder.
What this means for brands
A brand that adds content to the feed is competing with everything else in it. A brand that earns its place in a trusted, curated environment is operating in an entirely different register.
The gatekeeper who matters now is not an institution — it is a voice with a genuine community, a track record in a specific domain, and the reach to carry a message beyond its immediate audience. In the NZ automotive sector, that voice has existed for more than thirty years — built around audiences who chose to follow, not because an algorithm served them content they might tolerate, but because they trust what they find there.
For a brand wanting to reach those audiences, that trust is not incidental. It is the whole point.
